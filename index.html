A management playbook for AI assistance

When should AI help humans — and how much?

Every human-in-the-loop AI system must answer two questions at every moment: should the system signal, and how prescriptively? A hint preserves learning; a full solution replaces effort. More signals can improve today's decision while eroding tomorrow's. This site translates the findings of Dynamic AI Signal Policy Design into a practical framework for students and managers.

The central tension

AI assistance is rarely free. More frequent or more prescriptive signals can substantially improve the current decision but can also increase alert fatigue, reduce cognitive effort, and erode skill retention. Once assistance shapes future capacity, signal design becomes a dynamic problem — and the usual tools mislead.

1

Immediate gain

A stronger signal can flip a current decision from wrong to right — higher accuracy, faster resolution, fewer local errors.

2

Efficacy drift

Repeated assistance can silently change the system's future capacity to improve decisions: fatigued clinicians ignore alerts, trainees stop practicing, operators lose vigilance.

3

The design problem

Choose signal intensity over a horizon to maximize cumulative decision quality subject to a cap on cumulative downstream cost. This is the Signal-Policy Design (SPD) problem.

The three-regime rule

The paper shows that the right design depends on two things: how materially signaling shifts future efficacy (drift), and how credibly you can specify its downstream cost. That yields three operational regimes.

Low drift

Myopic is enough

When signals barely shift future efficacy, just pick the best signal for the current decision. The benchmarked gain from dynamic design is under 2%.

Material drift, full spec

SPD or RD

When drift matters and you can credibly specify a full cost schedule, direct SPD is the oracle. Relative Design (RD) retains 97–99.7% of its value with far less fragile inputs.

Material drift, partial spec

RD is the object

Under burden-misspecification or order-breaking proxy noise, plug-in SPD can go wrong. RD regularizes the design problem — it's not a fallback, it's the right object.

What this replaces. Confidence-gating, accuracy thresholds, and "show alert when p > 0.8" rules are all flavors of the myopic benchmark. They're fine in low-drift settings. In high-drift settings they systematically overuse assistance and erode the very capacity that makes the system work.
