Dynamic AI Signal Policy Design
Stefanos Poulidis
INSEAD, Decision Sciences — stefanos.poulidis@insead.edu
Working paper, 2026 · Formatted for Management Science submission
Read the PDF → Copy citation
Abstract

Human-in-the-loop AI systems often decide not only whether to signal a decision-maker, but how much assistance to provide over time. More frequent or more prescriptive signals — e.g., solutions rather than hints — can substantially improve current decisions but can also increase alert fatigue and reduce cognitive effort, eroding future performance and skill retention.

We study a constrained signal-policy design problem (SPD) in a finite-horizon Markov decision process in which a planner chooses signal intensity for a human operator. We show that reduced-form transition kernels are structurally insufficient for signal design because they do not identify whether downstream consequences arise from action-mediated displacement or from changes in future efficacy — the system's state-dependent capacity to improve subsequent decisions, conditional on the realized action. To recover these design-relevant components, we introduce a factored-kernel representation that separates behavioral response, physical transitions, and efficacy drift.

This structure yields a clear design logic for dynamic signal design: use direct SPD under full specification, and use a structured relative-design (RD) when the planner can evaluate marginal signal upgrades without fully specifying downstream costs across states; under RD, stronger signals are used when their incremental benefit justifies their downstream cost. We formalize this principle by exactly characterizing the optimal penalized ordered-menu policy through a single shadow price.

Computational studies in healthcare and engineering reveal a three-regime pattern: when efficacy drift is low, myopic rules based on immediate rewards are nearly optimal; when drift is material and the model is correctly specified, RD retains nearly all of SPD's value; under misspecification, RD can improve realized performance by regularizing the design problem. Our framework provides a disciplined basis for the design and governance of AI signal policies.

Headline results

  1. Decision-insufficiency of reduced-form evidence (Prop. 3.1, Cor. 3.1). The same reduced-form continuation data can support opposite local signal rankings depending on how signal dependence is allocated between behavioral response and efficacy drift.
  2. IDE decomposition (Lemma 3.2). The value of a signal change splits into immediate, displacement, and efficacy channels. Any credible design must track all three.
  3. Ordered-menu threshold policy (Theorem 3.1). Once RD is the selected object, the optimal penalized policy admits an exact characterization through a single shadow price λ*.
  4. Safe harbor (Prop. 3.3). Under signal-independent efficacy, the drift channel is absent and myopic rules are optimal.
  5. Near-tie screen (Prop. 3.4). Continuation effects only overturn myopic rankings in the neighborhood of myopic ties.
  6. Full-horizon performance guarantee (Prop. 3.5). The one-step approximation comes with a bounded loss relative to the exact dynamic optimum.
  7. Three-regime deployment rule (§4). Low drift → myopic; material drift + full spec → SPD ≈ RD (97.9%–99.7% retention); material drift + misspecification → RD can outperform plug-in SPD by regularization.

About this site

This is a pedagogical companion to the paper, aimed at INSEAD MBA students and practitioners deploying human–AI decision-support systems. All interactive elements (the simulator, the diagnostic wizard) are intentionally small and transparent — the tabular MDP in the simulator is designed for intuition, not research claims.
